A Bilingual Society

The Basque Country is an autonomous zone in nothern Spain. It is unique in that it harbours a bilingual population. People speak the national language of Spanish as well as their regional language of Basque. Unlike most regional languages in Spain, Basque is completely unrelated to Spanish, predating its advent. The Study

In 2017, a study was conducted on linguistic ability in the Basque Country. The study, while localised to this single region, can provide insights into the impact of bilingualism on a society. Given that over 20% of Australians speak a second language, these findings could provide commentary on the future evolution of Australian.

The study featured people from several different Basque speaking regions. The map to the right details where the test subject frequently came from.

Percentage of Test Subjects from Each Region


Linguistic Testing

Several tests were conducted to capture linguistic ability. Of these tests, the most definitive was the LexTALE test which involves the subject having to determine whether a given word exists in the language or not. Scores range from 0-100 with a score of 70 typically demonstrating fluency.

The plot to the left shows that the population is consistently proficient at Spanish, while displaying more mixed results in Basque. Additionally, we see that native bilinguals are typically more proficient at both languages than native monolinguals, implying a relationship between age of acquisition and proficiency.


Second Language Acquisition

Given that the population is consistently proficient at Spanish, we can focus in on the proficiency of English and Basque. By observing the distribution of Basque scores amongst the populations of fluent and non-fluent English seperately, we see that fluent English speakers are typically more proficient at Basque as well. This suggests that perhaps being raised bilingual increases one's aptitude for acquiring other languages.


Age of Acquisition

We can further explore the link between a subject's proficiency in a language and the age at which they acquired it. We do this by observing what percentage of subjects have acquired each of Spanish, English and Basque by each age.

We see that filtering for higher perfomers leads to steeper curves in Spanish and Basque. This suggests that acquiring a language younger increases one's likely proficiency. English however, is an exception to this pattern, showing instead the opposite trend.

Language and Education

While we have explored the distribution of linguistic ability, we are yet to explore the impacts of linguistic ability on an individual's life. We can do this by exploring the link between educational level and linguistic proficiency. The below charts demonstrate that across both Basque and English, higher linguistic ability seems to correlate with higher education level. This suggests that being proficient in a second language may better equip you with the learning agility for higher education.
